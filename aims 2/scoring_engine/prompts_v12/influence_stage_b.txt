# Classification Rules

**Power Imagery (PowIm)** is scored if some person or group of persons in the story is concerned about establishing, maintaining, or restoring their power — that is, their impact, control, or influence — over another person, group of persons, or the world at large.

There are THREE criteria — only ONE needs to be met:
1. **Power Actions**: Strong forceful actions affecting others, unsolicited help/advice/teaching, controlling others, persuading/convincing/arguing to win (not to reach agreement), trying to impress others or gain fame/recognition.
2. **Emotional Impact**: Someone's actions arouse STRONG positive or negative emotions in others (delight, awe, gratitude, fear, anger).
3. **Reputation/Position Concern**: Someone shows concern for reputation, position, or what others think of their power.

**Calibration (V10 dissertation cohort)**: ~29% of stories from this cohort have PowIm=1, ~71% have PowIm=0. Default toward PowIm=0 on borderline cases unless rubric criteria are clearly met.

# What Does NOT Qualify

- Truly routine role behavior (teacher teaching routine class, soldier pitching a tent, captain providing routine cooperative information)
- Romantic gestures and generosity (arranging serenades, special dinners) — affiliative
- Job anxiety without active power pursuit (passive worry about job loss)
- Achievement without recognition concern (e.g., scientific work focused on solving the puzzle, not on impact)
- Solicited advice without further power elaboration
- Casual discussions, routine explanations
- Arguments that resolve mutually without anyone trying to win
- Routine helping
- Authority figure AFFIRMING the other person's autonomous choices (vs DIRECTING them)

# Key Distinctions for Borderline Cases

1. **Routine vs. Consequential Role Behavior**: A captain enforcing rules with real consequences (denying boarding, reprimanding) IS Power. A captain providing cooperative routine information is NOT.
2. **Internal-Achievement vs. Public-Impact Scientific Work**: Scientists absorbed in the technical puzzle = NOT Power. Scientists framing their work in terms of fame, public benefit, or recognition = Power.
3. **Routine Performance vs. Audience-Impact Performance**: Performers doing their routine show = NOT Power. Performers explicitly trying to gain audience awe / publicity = Power.
4. **Authority Affirming vs. Directing**: Father affirming daughter's choice = NOT Power. Father unilaterally directing daughter's decisions = Power.
5. **Private Craft vs. Public Display**: Architect designing a small house for self-satisfaction = NOT Power. Architect designing a landmark for fame and critical recognition = Power.
6. **Mutual Conversation vs. Domination**: Couple sharing affiliatively = NOT Power. One partner pressuring/dominating the other's decisions = Power.
7. **Solicited vs. unsolicited**: Was the help/advice requested? If yes, is there evidence the advisor goes BEYOND what was asked?
8. **Achievement vs. Power**: Is the concern about doing something WELL (achievement) or about having IMPACT on others (power)?

# Decision Framework

When providers are split, ask:
1. Can I identify a SPECIFIC power goal (impact, control, influence)?
2. Does at least one character show CONCERN about IMPACT, CONTROL, or INFLUENCE?
3. Is this routine role behavior or consequential power?
4. Does the dissertation-cohort calibration (29% PowIm) support borderline → PowIm=0?

If YES to 1, 2 and the actions are consequential → PowIm=1.
If routine role behavior with no consequential power assertion → PowIm=0.
